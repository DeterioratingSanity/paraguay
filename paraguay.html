So - You want to go to Paraguay?
It's... not about to be a fast trip. Assuming you mean to arrive directly in Asuncion, you had better pack a neck pillow and an ambien (barring you start already in South America, or in Madrid) - because not many aircraft go there.
Luckily, you can just stop over in Panama City, and make from New York to Asuncion in somewhere between 12 and 18 hours. If you make the flight with Panama's flag airline, Compania Panamena de Aviacion, or Copa, then the trip will cost about $350 - If you have to stop in Bogota, too, the flight could take you 35 or more hours! Here's where you'll be going - the Silvio Pettirossi International Airport. It sits in Luque, which is technically its own place, but logistically is a city inside of Asucion's metropolitan area. Luque isn't unique though - 20 cities hang out in that little bubble, along with ~30% of Paraguayans.
It used to be named after Alfredo Stroessner, a guy who thought being a dictator was a very fun passtime, and is now named for an Italian immigrant who started an aviation club and passed when the wings fell off his plane while he performed a stunt... oops.

For the first fellow, it turns out that leading a military coup and becoming supreme leader for over 30 years, but not treating your army well, tends to result in another military coup leaving you in a less than pleasant position. He survived, though. Indeed, Mr Stroessner hid in Brazil for 17 years, until his guts got moldy because of pneumonia. Unfortunately, Mr Pettirossi wasn't so lucky.

He had a relatively interesting life, learning how to fly in France thanks to Paraguayan government scholarships prior to World War One. He got several flying records, bought a plane, and came home. Then, much as is stated above, he tricked too hard, the wings fell off his plane, and he fell into a cattle ranch, an accident he did not survive.

He got an airport named after him though... so I guess he did OK in the end.

All that being said, lets see about Paraguay's history!

Paraguay was originally populated by the Guarani - and because Paraguay is in the backwoods of South America, they managed to escape cultural genocide. And regular genocide. Basically they avoided genocide. Not to say they didn't have a bad time. The Guarani were basically run over with a truck before being set on fire, but the truck of colonial / imperial Spain got a flat tire before they could reverse back over everybody... forceable Christianization tends to do that.

Beyond just the Christianization, you also have to contend with the issue of people only accepting new religion because the alternative was being enslaved. And sometimes, that was worth about as much as the paper clip on my boot is to an aggravated llama - that is, nothing. Slavers had a penchant for doing a little three step plan:
Wait for a Sunday, so everybody is close together
Burn down the monestary, and un-alive anybody who disagrees with you for doing that
Sell the rest.

Guarani society survived, though, because priests gave them guns on orders of the Pope - no, literally, so they could watch out for themselves.

Why is it called Paraguay? Why is it there? Why is the sky blue, rather than neon pink? These are all questions you might ask, but I can't answer them equally. Here is my best effort.

1. The Guarani words of "Feather Crown" (paragua) and "water" (y) - Combined to render "Feathered Crown of the Waters" - as there are rivers about.
2. Plate tectonics.
3. Because I said so?

Because its named for the native words, its pretty much always been called Paraguay, whether it was a principality, a governorate, a state, or whatever else in the rigamarole of the Spanish colonial bureaucracy. It has sometimes been lumped in with the "Governorate of the Rio de la Plata" - basically, everywhere near this river that gives me silver, which I like, for the Spanish.

Paraguay is about 406k square kilomiters, 2.6% of which is water - as one might guess, being that Paraguay is very wholly land-locked. Good job for having that much water at all, dry dry country.

It is very hot sometimes, but it doesn't have seasons, just wet and dry. Through its Spring and Summer, winds blow northward, whereas its Fall and Winter bring cooler Andean winds back South.

It is the largest city in Paraguay, with 3 million out of Paraguays entire population (just 7 million, total) living in the city itself, or in its metropolitan area!



Asuncion sits in the crook between rivers, on the border between Paraguay and Argentina. Rather than existing in another governmental state, it is an independently controlled city, much as Washington, D.C. is in the United States.
The Paraguay River for which Paraguay as a nation is named is only half the length of the longest river in Paraguay, the Parana River.
The Parana travels 4900 kilometers south, crossing Paraguay and dipping into Argentina - I think for travel food, but maybe just because of the currents in that area. Along its length, the Parana plays jigsaw, merging with the eponymous rivers of Uruguay and Paraguay, until it becomes what the Spanish called the Rio de la Plata, for all the silver that came down it for them.

The culture of Paraguay is the culture of the Guarani (mixed with some immigrant stuff - both the original Spanish moving in and taking over the place, and later Italian and generic European and African immigration after Paraguay was a thing that existed on the radars of the wider world, rather than just the weird woods with strange fellows who are not Europeans*). Guarani villages shared households, and formed their own dialecets with which they identified as a group. They have star gods who rule broad concepts like night and existence, animistic dieties anthropomorphizing emotions , sentiments, and such. Then there are the Pombero goblins and their leader Jasy who likes drowning children and eating honey... Winnie the Pooh?
Guarani, before encountering Europeans, wore very little, largely because it was catastrophically hot, and only British regulars wear wool uniforms in tropical weather. If your culture evolves in the tropics, it overcomes the mandate upon trousers with a remarkable speed. Women wore, and still sometimes wear, black body paints, whereas men wear red and black paints. Some wore earrings and lip rings fashioned from gold or shell, and religious charms produced from the hair of close family were sometimes worn tied around the limbs.
Guarani also adopted Peninsular styles, adopting the more common guise of Latin American inhabitants - though their culture remains vibrant.

This is the flag of Paraguay
The red/white/blue tricolor is based on that of Revolutionary and Contemporary France, representing liberty and indpendence, both things which the recently oppressed state in South America essaying at not being provincial property of the Spanish government.

The front of the flag has a laurel wreath surrounding a gold star, whereas the back carries the seal of the Paraguayan treasury - a lion carrying a red cap, the design of which is common to several in classical / ancient Greece.

One importat holiday and celibration in Paraguay falls on the 29th of September every year. It celebrates the Paraguayan victory in the first battle of the Chaco War, a conflict between Bolivia and Paraguay over the Chaco region, which is today divided between Bolivia, Paraguay, and Argentina, though 2/3 of the disputed area went to Paraguay. The celebration, Boqueron Battle Victory Day, remembers the end of the first battle of the war.
The foremost annual festival in Paraguay is held in the honor of the patron saint of Paraguay, a fellow named St. Blaise (Blas). According to Catholics and the internet, he is in charge of wild animals - I guess rebellion likened thereto, and its why native populations who converted liked him. Basically everybody celebrates (as ~88% population is Catholic) - and there are massive parties rife with dancing, parades, and church services - the latter of course having a bit less party overall.
Another widely celebrated holiday is the anniversary of the founding of Asuncion - because whatever you think of colonialism, a day off work is a day off work! On the 15th of August, it remembers the firt Spanish fellows to doodle their way up the Paraguay River to each what is now Asuncion.

The guy in charge of that group as named Juan de Salazar etc., whose parents gave him somewhere in the neighborhood of far too many names. It was named for the feast day in the Catholic calendar which also fell on the 15th. As it was founded in 1541, it is a very very old city (duh) but is also one of the oldest surviving Spanish American cities (less duh, because reconquest, and also arson).
